# Brief Methodology

## Purpose

This document captures a general methodology for writing strong creator briefs.

It is intentionally product-agnostic. The goal is to describe what a brief is for, what information it should be built from, what affects its quality, and how to evaluate whether it is good.

This is not a product specification. It is a reusable method for thinking about briefs across campaigns, creators, and content formats.

---

## 1. What A Brief Is

A brief is an execution document.

It is not just a collection of campaign notes, and it is not just a legal or operational checklist.

A good brief translates business goals, product truth, creator context, and execution constraints into something a creator can actually use to make content.

At minimum, a brief should answer four questions:

1. Why are we making this content?
2. What should the creator actually show or say?
3. What boundaries or constraints matter?
4. What does successful delivery look like?

If the document cannot answer those four questions clearly, it is not yet a strong brief.

---

## 2. What A Brief Should Be Optimized For

A strong brief should be optimized for:

- clarity
- usability
- truthfulness
- relevance
- execution quality

It should not be optimized for:

- internal completeness at the expense of readability
- excessive brand language
- unnecessary technical detail
- forcing creators into rigid scripts

The best brief is not the one with the most information. It is the one that gives the creator enough information to make good content with minimal confusion.

---

## 3. The Core Inputs To A Brief

Every brief should be built from a small number of input categories.

### 3.1 Business Objective

This is the strategic purpose of the campaign.

Examples:

- drive sign-ups
- increase qualified traffic
- support a product launch
- educate the market
- build brand awareness
- promote a specific workflow or feature

The brief should reflect the real objective, not a vague proxy.

### 3.2 Product Reality

The brief must stay anchored in product truth.

This includes:

- what the product can actually do
- what can realistically be shown
- what the creator can reasonably test
- what claims are safe
- what limitations matter

If this layer is weak, the brief becomes high-risk or artificial.

### 3.3 Audience Context

The brief should define the intended viewer not only by category, but by need.

Useful audience inputs include:

- who they are
- what they are trying to do
- how they do it today
- what pain points they face
- what objections they may have
- what would make them care

### 3.4 Creator Context

The same campaign should often be framed differently for different creators.

Useful creator inputs include:

- platform
- niche
- style
- audience expectations
- typical narrative structure
- what they can explain or demonstrate naturally

### 3.5 Content Core / Use Case

This is the heart of the brief.

It should define:

- what specific use case to show
- what problem it solves
- how it should be demonstrated
- what outcome the audience should understand
- what details or proof points will make it credible

### 3.6 Strategic Framing

This shapes how the content enters the conversation.

Examples:

- trend hook
- comparison angle
- free angle
- outcome angle
- workflow angle

This layer should support the use case, not overshadow it.

### 3.7 Execution Constraints

This includes:

- platform
- duration
- review process
- deadlines
- assets
- link requirements
- CTA requirements

### 3.8 Commercial And Legal Constraints

This includes:

- disclosure
- tracking links
- promo rules
- payment terms
- license rights
- retention requirements
- exclusivity terms

---

## 4. What Should Influence Brief Content

Not every input should influence the brief equally.

### 4.1 Direct Content Drivers

These should directly shape what the brief says:

- business objective
- product reality
- selected use case
- audience need
- creator context
- execution constraints
- legal and commercial requirements

### 4.2 Framing Drivers

These should affect how the brief is written, but not replace the core substance:

- campaign theme
- trend hook
- timing
- competitive context
- creator style preference

### 4.3 Things That Should Not Drive The Creator-Facing Brief

These may matter internally, but should not directly shape the outward brief:

- internal field names
- implementation details
- internal tool or preset labels
- backend paths
- unnecessary technical jargon

If a creator cannot use the information to make better content, it probably does not belong in the brief.

---

## 5. Input Priority When Things Conflict

When inputs conflict, use the following priority order:

1. legal truth
2. product truth
3. campaign objective
4. selected use case
5. audience need
6. creator fit
7. framing / hook preference
8. nice-to-have brand wording

This ensures that:

- compliance is never weakened by creative preference
- product truth is never weakened by campaign hype
- use case clarity is not sacrificed for trend chasing

## 6. Who Should Provide What

### 6.1 Campaign / Marketing Owner

Should provide:

- business objective
- primary CTA
- campaign success metric
- timing or launch context
- strategic positioning

### 6.2 Product / PM

Should provide:

- what the product can actually do
- recommended use case path
- what is easy or hard to demonstrate
- what should not be overstated

### 6.3 Creator Manager / Content Strategist

Should provide:

- creator fit
- channel-specific framing
- execution guidance
- style alignment

### 6.4 Operations / BD

Should provide:

- deadlines
- review rules
- tracking links
- promo codes if applicable
- payment structure

### 6.5 Legal / Brand

Should provide:

- disclosure language
- licensing boundaries
- claim guardrails
- high-risk wording to avoid

### 6.6 Research / Growth / Customer Teams

Should provide:

- audience insights
- pain points
- objections
- patterns from real user behavior

### 6.7 Creator

Should provide:

- what they actually tested
- what feels natural in their voice
- what they can realistically demonstrate
- what would feel forced or unconvincing

---

## 7. How To Gather The Right Inputs

### 7.1 Business Objective

Gather from:

- campaign kickoff docs
- direct campaign owner input
- launch plans
- growth targets

### 7.2 Product Reality

Gather from:

- product demos
- PM notes
- product documentation
- internal walkthroughs
- hands-on testing

### 7.3 Audience Context

Gather from:

- user research
- interviews
- support feedback
- sales conversations
- creator audience observation
- comment and community analysis

### 7.4 Creator Context

Gather from:

- recent content review
- creator profile
- prior collaborations
- creator manager notes

### 7.5 Strategic Framing

Gather from:

- current campaign theme
- market trends
- competitive messaging
- timing-sensitive narrative opportunities

### 7.6 Use Case Content

Gather from:

- product teams
- prior examples
- use case pages
- real demos
- successful scripts or content outputs

### 7.7 Execution Constraints

Gather from:

- operations docs
- project trackers
- asset packs
- review and delivery process

### 7.8 Commercial / Legal Inputs

Gather from:

- contracts
- legal-approved guidance
- brand safety policies
- standard creator agreement terms

## 8. Recommended Structure Of A Good Brief

While different teams may format briefs differently, a strong brief usually follows a similar logical order.

### 8.1 Campaign Objective

- what this campaign is trying to achieve

### 8.2 Audience

- who the content is for
- why they should care

### 8.3 Core Message

- how the product should be described
- what idea the viewer should leave with

### 8.4 Use Case

- what is being demonstrated
- who it is for
- what problem it solves
- how to show it
- what viewers should take away

### 8.5 Hook / Framing

- recommended opening direction
- topical angle
- optional comparison or trend entry point

### 8.6 CTA

- what the viewer should do
- where the CTA should appear
- what link or code must be used

### 8.7 Assets / References

- example links
- demo links
- visual guidance
- approved references

### 8.8 Guardrails

- claims to avoid
- disclosure requirements
- messaging boundaries

### 8.9 Delivery Rules

- format
- duration
- deadlines
- revision process

### 8.10 Commercial Terms

- payment
- licensing
- retention
- exclusivity if relevant

## 9. How To Evaluate A Good Brief

Use these dimensions when reviewing any brief.

### 9.1 Clarity

Can someone quickly understand what this content is about and why it exists?

### 9.2 Executability

Can the creator realistically draft and shoot from this brief without major confusion?

### 9.3 Truthfulness

Does the brief stay inside product reality and avoid claims that are too broad or too hard to verify?

### 9.4 Relevance

Is the brief genuinely tailored to the creator, platform, audience, and campaign?

### 9.5 Persuasiveness

Does the brief set the creator up to make content that can influence the intended viewer action?

### 9.6 Risk Control

Does the brief reduce compliance, legal, and messaging risk?

### 9.7 Readability

Is the document easy to scan and use, or overloaded with internal or low-value detail?

---

## 10. A Simple Review Checklist

Before approving a brief, check the following:

### 10.1 Objective

- Is the campaign objective explicit?
- Is the CTA goal explicit?

### 10.2 Product Reality

- Are all major claims supportable?
- Is the demo path realistic?

### 10.3 Use Case

- Is there at least one concrete use case?
- Does it explain how to show it?
- Does it explain what outcome the viewer should understand?

### 10.4 Audience Fit

- Is the audience specific enough?
- Is the pain point clear?

### 10.5 Creator Fit

- Does the brief feel suited to this creator’s style and format?
- Would the creator naturally say or show these things?

### 10.6 Hook Fit

- Does the opening angle connect naturally to the body of the content?
- Is the hook supporting the use case rather than distracting from it?

### 10.7 CTA Fit

- Is the CTA natural?
- Is the link provided?
- Is placement clear?

### 10.8 Operational Completeness

- Are deadlines clear?
- Are review rules clear?
- Are payment / license / retention terms clear?

### 10.9 Asset Completeness

- Are links and assets actually present?
- Are there any empty placeholders?
- Are any references unusable?

### 10.10 Risk

- Are there any likely overclaims?
- Is disclosure covered?
- Are any internal terms leaking into the creator-facing brief?

## 11. Common Failure Modes

These are the most common reasons briefs fail.

### 11.1 Too Vague

The brief says what the brand wants, but not what the creator should actually do.

### 11.2 Too Generic

The brief could apply to any creator and therefore does not help this specific creator much.

### 11.3 Too Technical

The brief explains systems or features in internal language instead of creator-usable language.

### 11.4 Too Promotional

The brief pushes language that sounds unnatural or forces unearned certainty.

### 11.5 Hook / Body Mismatch

The opening angle promises one thing, while the main use case demonstrates something else.

### 11.6 Operational Gaps

Important practical details are missing, such as links, codes, assets, or timing.

### 11.7 Internal Leakage

The brief exposes internal names, labels, paths, or implementation details that do not help the creator.

---

## 12. Final Principle

The purpose of a brief is not to document everything the team knows.

The purpose of a brief is to convert the right knowledge into a creator-usable execution document.

That means:

- truth over hype
- use case over feature list
- guidance over overload
- creator usability over internal completeness

When in doubt, a brief should become more concrete, more usable, and more honest.
