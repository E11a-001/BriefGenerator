# Brief Execution Framework

## Purpose

This document turns our recent discussion into a reusable framework for writing, reviewing, and improving creator briefs.

The goal is not to make briefs longer. The goal is to make briefs more usable:

- clear enough for a creator to understand quickly
- concrete enough for a creator to execute
- controlled enough to reduce claim and compliance risk
- flexible enough to adapt to different campaigns, products, and creators

---

## 1. What A Brief Must Do

A good brief should answer four questions clearly:

1. Why are we making this piece of content?
2. What exactly should the creator show or say?
3. What boundaries or constraints matter?
4. What counts as a successful delivery?

If a brief does not answer those four questions, it is incomplete, even if it contains a lot of information.

---

## 2. Core Inputs To A Brief

These are the information groups that should feed into a brief.

### 2.1 Business Goal

This defines why the campaign exists.

Examples:

- drive sign-ups
- increase qualified traffic
- educate the market
- improve awareness
- support a launch
- push adoption of a specific feature or workflow

Without a clear business goal, the brief cannot prioritize what matters most.

### 2.2 Product Reality

The brief must be based on what the product can actually do, what can actually be shown, and what can actually be claimed.

This includes:

- stable product capabilities
- realistic setup flow
- realistic demo path
- known limitations
- claims that should be avoided

If this layer is weak, the brief will create overclaim risk or force the creator into artificial scripts.

### 2.3 Target Audience

The brief should define who the content is for, not just in demographic terms but in problem terms.

Useful audience inputs include:

- who they are
- what they are trying to accomplish
- how they solve it today
- what frustrates them
- what would make them care
- what they are skeptical about

### 2.4 Creator Context

A brief should be adapted to the creator, not just to the campaign.

Useful creator inputs include:

- creator niche
- channel style
- tone of voice
- usual content format
- audience expectations
- what the creator can realistically film or explain well

### 2.5 Campaign Strategy

This determines how the content enters the conversation.

Examples:

- trend hook
- launch hook
- comparison angle
- free angle
- efficiency angle
- workflow angle

This layer should influence the opening and framing, but not override the core use case.

### 2.6 Use Case / Content Core

This is the heart of the brief.

It should define:

- what the creator is actually demonstrating
- what problem it solves
- how to show it
- what viewers should understand afterward
- what proof or examples should appear

### 2.7 Execution Constraints

These are practical delivery rules such as:

- platform
- video length
- timeline
- review rounds
- asset usage
- link placement
- promo code
- CTA requirements

### 2.8 Commercial / Legal Requirements

These include:

- disclosure rules
- tracking links
- promo rules
- license terms
- posting retention
- exclusivity
- payment terms

These matter, but they should not dominate the entire brief.

## 3. What Should Directly Affect Brief Content

Not every input should influence the brief in the same way.

### 3.1 Primary Drivers

These should directly shape the content of the brief:

- business goal
- core product reality
- selected use case(s)
- target audience
- creator context
- execution constraints
- commercial / legal requirements

### 3.2 Secondary Drivers

These should shape the framing, but not become the core of the brief:

- campaign trend hook
- market timing
- creator-specific style preferences
- historical performance insights

### 3.3 Things That Should Not Lead The Brief

These may exist internally but should not drive outward-facing creator copy:

- internal field names
- internal workflow labels
- implementation details
- product architecture language
- technical metrics that do not help the creator shoot better content

Examples of low-value or risky brief content:

- internal preset names
- backend path references
- code-level system descriptions
- unverifiable performance claims

---

## 4. Priority Order For Brief Inputs

When two inputs conflict, use this priority order:

1. Commercial / legal truth
2. Product truth
3. Campaign objective
4. Selected use case(s)
5. Creator context
6. Framing / trend hook
7. Nice-to-have brand wording

This means:

- a trend hook should not force the brief away from the real use case
- a nice brand phrase should never override product truth
- creator style matters, but not more than compliance or product reality

## 5. Who Should Provide Each Input

### 5.1 Marketing / Campaign Owner

Should provide:

- campaign objective
- primary CTA
- success metric
- campaign theme or timing
- strategic hook

### 5.2 Product / PM

Should provide:

- real capability boundaries
- realistic use case paths
- stable demo flow
- claims that are safe or unsafe

### 5.3 Creator Manager / Content Strategist

Should provide:

- creator fit
- platform adaptation
- suggested content format
- creator-friendly phrasing direction

### 5.4 Operations / BD

Should provide:

- tracking links
- promo code if applicable
- review process
- deadlines
- payment terms

### 5.5 Legal / Brand

Should provide:

- disclosure rules
- usage rights
- claim guardrails
- sensitive or banned wording

### 5.6 Research / Growth / CS

Should provide:

- audience pain points
- objections
- patterns from real users
- what messages resonate or fail

### 5.7 Creator

Should provide:

- realistic filming constraints
- preferred narrative style
- what they actually tested
- what felt natural or unnatural in use

## 7. Recommended Brief Structure

A good general-purpose brief should follow this structure.

### 7.1 Campaign Objective

- what this content is trying to achieve
- what matters most

### 7.2 Audience

- who the content is for
- what problem or need they have

### 7.3 Core Message

- how the product should be described
- what should feel true after watching

### 7.4 Primary Use Case(s)

For each use case:

- what it is
- who it is for
- what problem it solves
- how to show it
- what outcome viewers should understand
- suggested proof points or elements
- reference links

### 7.5 Hook / Framing Suggestions

- trend hook
- narrative entry point
- comparison angle
- what not to overemphasize

### 7.6 CTA Guidance

- where CTA should appear
- what the CTA should achieve
- what link or code must be used
- example phrasing if needed

### 7.7 Assets / Resources

- links
- visuals
- demo environment
- cover guidance
- external references

### 7.8 Guardrails

- claims to avoid
- messaging boundaries
- required disclosure

### 7.9 Delivery Rules

- platform
- duration
- deadlines
- review flow

### 7.10 Commercial / Legal Terms

- payment
- license
- retention
- exclusivity

## 8. Evaluation Dimensions For A Good Brief

Use these dimensions to judge whether a brief is good.

### 8.1 Clarity

Can the creator quickly understand:

- what this content is about
- why it exists
- what they need to show

### 8.2 Executability

Can the creator realistically film the content using the brief alone, without asking basic follow-ups?

### 8.3 Truthfulness

Does the brief stay within product reality and avoid pushing unverified or inflated claims?

### 8.4 Relevance

Does the brief fit this specific creator, platform, audience, and campaign?

### 8.5 Persuasiveness

Does the brief help the creator produce content that can actually move the intended outcome?

### 8.6 Risk Control

Does the brief reduce compliance, legal, messaging, and claim risk?

### 8.7 Readability

Is the document easy to scan, or is it overloaded with low-value detail?

## 9. Brief Review Checklist

Use this checklist before approving a brief.

### 9.1 Objective Check

- Is the campaign goal explicit?
- Is the primary CTA explicit?
- Is success directionally measurable?

### 9.2 Product Truth Check

- Is every major claim supportable?
- Is the demo path realistic?
- Are limitations or caveats handled appropriately?

### 9.3 Audience Check

- Is the audience specific enough?
- Is the pain point clear?
- Is the benefit framed in audience language, not internal product language?

### 9.4 Use Case Check

- Does the brief define at least one concrete use case?
- Does each use case explain how to show it?
- Does each use case explain what viewers should understand after watching?
- Are supporting proof points or showcase elements specific and helpful?

### 9.5 Hook Check

- Is the opening angle clear?
- Does the hook connect naturally to the use case?
- Does the hook risk overselling technical or peripheral details?

### 9.6 CTA Check

- Is the CTA natural for the creator’s style?
- Is the tracking link provided?
- Are placement instructions clear?
- Is the CTA aligned to campaign intent?

### 9.7 Compliance Check

- Is sponsorship disclosure covered?
- Are usage / posting rules covered?
- Are revision expectations covered?
- Are legal or licensing requirements present?

### 9.8 Asset Check

- Are reference links included?
- Are assets or cover instructions actually usable?
- Are there any empty placeholders?
- Are all externally referenced items accessible?

### 9.9 Creator Experience Check

- Does the brief feel like guidance rather than a forced script?
- Does it preserve creator voice?
- Does it avoid internal jargon?
- Could the creator take this and start drafting immediately?

---

## 10. Scoring Framework

Use a simple 1-5 score on each dimension:

- 1 = poor
- 2 = weak
- 3 = acceptable
- 4 = strong
- 5 = excellent

Recommended scoring categories:

- clarity
- executability
- truthfulness
- relevance
- persuasiveness
- risk control
- readability

Interpretation:

- 30+ = strong brief
- 24-29 = workable but should be improved
- below 24 = likely to create confusion, weak content, or revision waste

## 11. Common Failure Modes

Watch for these patterns.

### 11.1 Goal Ambiguity

The brief contains information but never clearly says what the content is for.

### 11.2 Feature Dumping

The brief describes many product capabilities but never turns them into a specific creator-facing use case.

### 11.3 Hook / Use Case Mismatch

The opening angle promises one thing, but the actual body of the content demonstrates something else.

### 11.4 Overclaiming

The brief encourages language that is too absolute, too technical, or too hard to prove.

### 11.5 Creator Misfit

The content direction may make sense in theory but does not fit the creator’s style or audience.

### 11.6 Operational Gaps

Important execution information is missing:

- links
- deadlines
- CTA rules
- payment terms
- reference assets

### 11.7 Internal Leakage

The brief exposes internal language or implementation details that are not useful to the creator.

---

## 12. Working Principle

The best brief is not the one with the most filled-in fields.

The best brief is the one that gives a creator enough truth, direction, and structure to make good content with minimal confusion and minimal forced language.

That means:

- product truth over marketing flourish
- use case over feature list
- creator usability over internal completeness
- clear priorities over information overload
